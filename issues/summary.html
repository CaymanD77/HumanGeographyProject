Smart City as Anti-Planning in the UK

Key Terms

  1. Urban Experiments: This term refers to the creative and unconventional ways that cities test new ideas. Rather than sticking to more traditional and tested planning methods, these experiments often involve trial and error in real-world environments.
  2. Experimental Governance: This term refers to the style of governing that relies on trying out new methods and adjusting them along the way. In the case of smart cities, this often means projects are led by different groups working independently, instead of (as is done traditionally) following a large organized plan.
  3. Anti-Planning: This term refers to the critiques of traditional city planning methods. It suggests that smart city projects often resist the usual top-down planning approach. While this flexibility, at times, can lead to innovation, it also creates problems if there is no real organized structure to guide long-term goals.

Core Argument of the Article

The authors (Cowley and Caprotti) argue that smart city projects in the UK, like in Bristol, Manchester, and Milton Keynes, aren’t just about technology but are also about changing how cities are planned and governed. Instead of being controlled from the top down by government planners as we usually see, these smart city projects often emerge through experiments that are led by different groups such as universities, tech companies, and local organizations.

Cowley and Caprotti point out that this experimental approach is flexible but also risky. Most of these smart city projects that we see don't follow more traditional planning methods, they lack coordination and long-term strategy. We can see that in Bristol and Manchester, smart city initiatives grew out of various independent projects rather than a clear, city-wide plan. The authors warn that while this flexibility can be a good thing, at the same time, it results in fragmented policies, social inequalities, and environmental goals being overlooked. Without a solid plan behind the experiments, these smart city developments likely will not live up to their promises of improving sustainability or making cities better for everyone.

How This Argument Provides New Understanding

This article sheds light on the idea that smart cities aren’t just about tech upgrades — they’re also changing how cities develop and are run. Instead of the more traditional and careful process of mapping with clear strategies, many smart city projects are more like trial-and-error experiments.

This perspective leads us to think about:

  • Inconsistent Policies: Since smart city projects often operate independently, they can lead to uncoordinated or conflicting plans.
  • Social Inequality: Smart city developments may unintentionally leave out marginalized groups if there’s no clear effort to include them.
  • Environmental Risks: While smart cities claim to support sustainability, the lack of long-term strategy may limit their impact.

Through the introduction of anti-planning, Cowley and Caprotti challenge the idea that smart cities are always well-organized and thoughtfully designed. They encourage policymakers to balance experimentation with structured plans to help ensure that smart city projects improve urban life for everyone.
